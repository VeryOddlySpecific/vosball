### ~~Randy Fuller — 2B, San Antonio Missions (MIL)~~

Randy Fuller grew up in Huntington, New York, a harbor town on Long Island's North Shore where the salt air and water have shaped generations of hard-nosed, deliberate men. His father coached youth baseball for nearly two decades at Heckscher Park, a man who spent more time teaching ten-year-olds the difference between a ball and a strike than he ever did worrying about hits. That philosophy — patience at the plate, trust the count, make the pitcher come to you — got drilled into Randy before he could drive. His mother, a high school English teacher with an almost clinical attention to detail, reinforced the same lesson in different words: read carefully before you react. Growing up in that household produced a hitter who, at twenty-two years old, already owns one of the most advanced batting eyes in the Milwaukee system. The Brewers knew exactly what they were getting when they made him the second overall pick in the 2060 draft — a young man who had been taught, long before he ever stepped into a college batter's box, that the most dangerous thing a hitter can do is swing at a pitch he doesn't need to.

Fuller's time at Franklin Pierce University — a small Division II program tucked into the hills of southern New Hampshire — turned that philosophical patience into a statistical weapon. Scouts who made the trek to Rindge weren't there for the atmosphere; they were there because Fuller was posting numbers that looked like a misprint. He paired a plus-plus walk rate with legitimate over-the-fence power, drawing comparisons to a certain archetype of patient masher who doesn't need to chase because he knows the fastball is coming eventually. His contact grades trail his other tools — he'll swing through hard stuff up in the zone and can be beaten with velocity — but the underlying profile of 80-grade discipline and 80-grade power potential is rare enough that those concerns read as correctable rather than defining. His first professional summer in the Carolina League confirmed both the promise and the caveat: .251 average, but .420 on-base and 19 home runs in 55 games. The walks were real, the power was real, and the contact work is clearly still in progress.

The projection on Fuller is one of the more compelling in the organization. If the contact grade climbs even modestly toward his other tools, the ceiling is a middle-of-the-order masher — a guy who bats third or fourth, posts a .390 on-base percentage, and hits 35 home runs in a full major league season. Even if it doesn't fully develop, his floor is a highly valuable "three true outcomes" type who does enough damage when he connects and takes enough walks to remain a net positive at the top of most lineups. The $6.9 million signing bonus Milwaukee invested reflects their belief that the contact is a developmental question, not a fundamental flaw. At 6'4" and 215 pounds, there's still physical projection left in the frame, and his winter league performance — .467/.544/1.156 in 13 games after his first pro season — suggests the bat speed and pitch recognition are ahead of schedule. Fuller may be a year or two away, but the ceiling is genuine, and the floor is higher than most prospects at his age and level.

### ~~Garrett Skaggs — 2B, Altoona Curve (PIT)~~

Garrett Skaggs grew up in Ravenna, Ohio, a Portage County town that sits just east of the industrial shadow cast by Cleveland and Akron. Ravenna is the kind of place where Friday nights belong to high school football and weekends belong to whatever work needs doing, and the Skaggs family operated by those terms without complaint. His father spent years on the floor of a stamping plant, a man who measured worth by output and tolerated very little in the way of self-pity. What that environment produced in Garrett was visible from his first youth league appearance — a competitive fire that coaches still describe as rare, a refusal to lose that bordered on infectious, and a willingness to grind through the ugly parts of development that turns off players who grew up with more comfort. Pittsburgh saw that when they made him the third overall pick in the 2060 draft, betting that the tools underneath the rough edges were worth cultivating. When scouts talk about Skaggs, they use the word "competitor" more than any other, and in Ravenna that word carries real weight.

The honest evaluation of Skaggs is that his bat is a paradox: elite power and discipline buried inside a contact game that currently grades as fringe. His plate discipline profiles as plus, suggesting he understands the zone and hunts the right pitch; his power grades as potentially elite, meaning when he does make contact, the ball travels. But the contact rating itself is stuck at an unconventional level that projects without significant growth, creating a profile that will either look brilliant or maddening depending on which nights you catch him. At Double-A his .197 average and .322 on-base tell the full story — he walked enough to stay relevant while the hits came infrequently. His raw defensive skill that comes in flashes, paired with his grit, determination, and potential leadership ability is a genuine asset, however, that gives Pittsburgh something to build around regardless of how the offensive development unfolds.

The projection on Skaggs requires a philosophical choice. If you believe contact can be manufactured through professional instruction and mechanical refinement, the ceiling is a top-of-the-order threat who pairs a .360 on-base with 30 home runs and plus defense up the middle. If you believe the contact ceiling is fixed, the floor is a useful platoon bat who earns roster spots through walks, occasional power, and reliable defense. Pittsburgh clearly believes in the former, which is why they spent draft capital of that magnitude on a hitter with a current contact grade most organizations would pass on. The energy he brings every day — that contagious enthusiasm teammates feed off of — suggests his ceiling is as much about character as tools, and Garrett Skaggs represents a significant investment in both.

### ~~Jesus Uresti — 1B, Las Vegas Aviators (OAK)~~

Jesus Uresti came out of Santo Domingo in the Dominican Republic, a country that treats baseball not as a pastime but as a profession, a path, and for many families, the clearest route to a different life. The Uresti household understood this with unusual clarity — Jesus was identified early as a legitimate prospect, and the resources that typically don't exist in Santo Domingo neighborhoods were redirected toward his development with single-minded purpose. He didn't come up through the academy system slowly; he came up as a known quantity, a young first baseman with a powerful right-handed swing and the kind of barrel instincts that money can't teach. St. Louis initially signed him as an international free agent without the benefit of a draft slot (though later he would be traded to Oakland), which means they invested on pure belief, the kind of investment that either looks visionary or foolish within a few years depending entirely on what happens to the tools. What followed his signing was professional production that justified the enthusiasm: full-season offensive numbers at Triple-A that would make any organization stand up and pay attention.

The numbers Uresti put up in his age-22 season at the PCL level were legitimately impressive — .306/.363/.505 with 23 home runs and 85 RBI in 129 games. His contact grade checks in at 50 with a ceiling of 60, but his power is more interesting, with an unrestrained ceiling, suggesting there is still significant juice to unlock. The part of his profile that concerns evaluators is his plate discipline, where he still has work to do, currently a good measure below where scouts think he could be and his 30-BB/125-K ratio tells a story about a hitter who relies on making contact rather than controlling the zone. His profile as a first baseman only limits his defensive utility, and his hustle has been noted by coaches as inconsistent — a reputation for not always running out plays that can be dismissed as a quirk or can become a character issue depending on how it progresses.

Uresti's projection ultimately comes down to whether the eye can improve. The power is real — when it peaks, scouts project 40 home run potential in the majors — but a first baseman who doesn't walk and strikes out at a high rate needs to hit for enough average to justify the lineup spot. If the discipline develops, even to league average, the combination of plus contact potential and plus-plus raw power creates a legitimate middle-of-the-order profile. If it doesn't, he profiles as a streaky power bat whose stretches of dominance get interrupted by extended slumps when pitchers exploit the aggressive tendencies. Oakland has the patience to develop him, and his age-22 Triple-A performance suggests he's ahead of schedule. The ceiling is legitimate; the floor is a question his plate approach will answer in the next year or two.

### Jaylen Hood — SS, Toronto (FCL) Blue Jays (TOR)

Jaylen Hood grew up in Cupertino, California, a city so thoroughly defined by the technology industry that describing anything there as ordinary requires a disclaimer. His parents were both engineers — one in semiconductor design, the other in systems architecture — and the intellectual environment of the Hood household was more akin to a graduate seminar than a typical suburban home. Dinner conversations involved optimization problems and first-principles thinking; summers were spent at academic enrichment programs rather than just travel ball showcases. That Jaylen emerged from this environment as a legitimate baseball prospect says something about both the sport's universality and his own stubbornness about pursuing it. Toronto saw him at 19, made him the 22nd overall pick in the 2059 draft, and invested based on athletic tools that don't require a tech background to appreciate: explosive speed, a shortstop's arm, and raw power that shows up when the right pitch arrives. His coaches describe him as someone who appears perpetually deep in thought, processing information at a pace that occasionally looks like passivity but reads more accurately as deliberation.

Hood's profile is one of the most projectable on Toronto's prospect list, which is a polite way of saying that very little of what he might become is visible in his current grades. His contact sits at 35 with a ceiling of 60 — workable but requiring significant development. His power grades at 35 currently with 65 potential. His eye sits at 30 with 60 upside. What he brings to the table right now is athletic upside, a plus arm (55) that fits shortstop, and impressive speed (65) that creates value regardless of what the bat does. In 33 Florida Complex League games at age 20, he hit .234/.326/.508 with 9 home runs — the power flashing against inferior competition, the contact and discipline work still clearly in early stages. The swing has length that coaches are working to shorten, and his approach at the plate is still developing the intuition that turns raw talent into professional production.

The projection on Hood requires patience that Toronto appears willing to invest. If the contact climbs to its 60 ceiling and the eye develops in tandem, the result is a shortstop who contributes at every level of the offensive spectrum — speed on the bases, gap power that should develop into home run pop, and an on-base ability that makes him a legitimate leadoff or two-hole hitter. The athletic tools are the kind that don't disappear; the question is whether the intellectual tools Hood clearly possesses in other domains transfer to the batter's box in the form of pitch recognition and situational awareness. Scouts who have followed him closely note that he absorbs instruction quickly — unusual in complex league players — and his introspective nature may prove to be an asset rather than a liability when it comes to self-correction. The floor is an athletic backup shortstop. The ceiling, if it clicks, is a legitimate top-of-the-order weapon.

### Bobby Childers — 1B, Charleston RiverDogs (NYY)

Bobby Childers is from Killingly, Connecticut, a town of about 17,000 in the quiet northeastern corner of the state where the Quinebaug River runs through the old mill district and the economy has been quietly reconfiguring itself for decades. Killingly has the particular character of New England mill towns — modest, deliberate, unsentimental — and the Childers family reflected those values in the way they raised Bobby. His father worked in trades, his mother in healthcare, and what distinguished the household was less any particular ambition than a genuine interest in the people around them. Bobby absorbed that quality directly; teammates describe him as someone who never fails to make you feel heard, a listener who asks follow-up questions and remembers details months later. New York selected him 17th overall in the 2060 draft because they saw a large-framed first baseman with legitimate power upside and a left-handed swing that could be shaped into something special. The makeup, by all accounts, makes the development work easier.

Childers is a projection play in the truest sense of the term. His current contact grades sit at 35, but his ceiling is 60 — a significant jump that requires professional instruction to unlock. His power follows a similar trajectory: 35 current, 65 ceiling, with physical tools that suggest the ceiling is attainable as he fills out at 6'2". His eye grades at 40 with 60 potential, meaning the discipline hasn't yet caught up with his natural intelligence about the game. In 51 South Atlantic League games as a 21-year-old, he hit .246/.330/.402 with 7 home runs — the kind of line that doesn't excite anyone but doesn't discourage anyone either. The swing has raw elements that coaches love, particularly the leverage he generates in the zone, and his first-base defense is already ahead of schedule. His approach at the plate reflects his personality — he listens, adjusts, and applies feedback in ways that older minor leaguers often can't.

The projection on Childers is the kind of high-ceiling, long-runway story that requires a particular kind of organizational commitment. The Yankees have a history of developing first-base power bats through the system, and Childers fits the archetype they prefer: large-framed lefty with plus raw power and a high floor for defensive utility at the position. If the contact and eye grades develop even two-thirds of the way toward their ceilings, the offensive profile becomes a .270 hitter with 30-plus home run potential and enough walks to sustain an OBP in the .360 range — a legitimate middle-of-the-order first baseman. The floor is a useful lefty bat off the bench who provides pop against right-handed pitching in high-leverage situations. Given his approach and work ethic, the Yankees have reason to believe the ceiling is the more likely outcome.

### Jose Cruz — SS, Minnesota Twins (MIN)

Jose Cruz grew up in Desamparados, a densely populated district on the southern edge of San José, Costa Rica, where baseball has historically existed alongside soccer in the national sporting consciousness without quite claiming the same cultural primacy it enjoys in the Dominican Republic or Venezuela. What Desamparados lacks in baseball infrastructure it compensates for in competitive intensity — the players who emerge from that environment tend to be self-starters, kids who found the game on their own terms rather than being funneled through a polished academy. The Cruz family recognized early that Jose was absorbing baseball faster than his peers, adapting immediately to coaching and applying corrections with a speed that made him stand out in showcase settings. Minnesota signed him as an international free agent at 18, placing him directly on the 40-man roster's radar before he'd played a professional game — a signal of how much confidence the organization has in his tools and his learning curve. At 6'0" and 175 pounds, there is still physical projection left in a frame that will add strength over the next few years.

Cruz's profile is one of the most intriguing in the system precisely because so much remains undeveloped while the ceiling is so clearly visible. His contact currently sits at 35 with a ceiling of 60; his power grades at 25 now but projects to 70 as the physical maturity catches up; his eye rates at 40 with 80 upside — a potentially elite on-base skill that could anchor the top of a lineup for a decade. His speed at 55 plays well enough at shortstop, and his fielding grades are promising: 55 range, 55 arm, solid hands, suggesting he can remain at the position long-term rather than sliding to second base as he fills out. The personality note from scouts is telling — he picks up information quickly and applies it immediately, which is the highest compliment you can pay a teenager in a professional setting. The floor of his development still sits comfortably above average.

The projection on Cruz is, at 18, necessarily more aspirational than analytical, but the ingredients for stardom are visible in a way that justifies the organizational commitment. If the contact develops toward its 60 ceiling and the eye grows into the plus-plus tool it projects to be, you have a shortstop who can play at the top of a major league lineup indefinitely — a player who hits for average, gets on base, contributes power as his body fills out, and plays credible defense at a premium position. The comp that floats around Minnesota's front office is a more disciplined version of the dynamic shortstop archetype — athletic, smart, gifted with baseball instincts that can't be coached. That may be overstating it at this stage, but the tools are there. Cruz is perhaps three or four years away, but what he could become is one of the more compelling prospects in the organization.

### Tim van Berkel — 2B, Texas Rangers (TEX)

Tim van Berkel comes from Weesp, a small historic town on the Vecht River roughly fifteen kilometers southeast of Amsterdam, in a country where baseball occupies a curious corner of the national sporting culture — officially supported, genuinely competitive at the international level, but still playing second fiddle to football and cycling in the public imagination. What Weesp lacks in baseball tradition it more than compensates for in precision and discipline, and the van Berkel household operated accordingly. Tim's father coached junior baseball in the Dutch federation and approached the game with the methodical rigor that characterizes Dutch technical instruction — nothing wasted, every repetition purposeful, fundamentals before flair. Tim absorbed both the lessons and the work ethic with unusual completeness. When Texas signed him as an international free agent at 18, they were acquiring a tooled-up 6'2" middle infielder whose 80-grade running speed tests at the top of any scouting pool, regardless of nationality, paired with a mindset that coaches in the system immediately identified as a gift.

Van Berkel's profile is defined by the gap between his current and potential grades, which is as wide as any on Texas's list. His contact sits at 35 currently but projects to 65 — a two-grade jump that would transform his offensive identity. His eye grades at 40 now with 80 potential, suggesting the plate discipline hasn't yet developed the professional-level pitch recognition that his intelligence should eventually produce. His power is modest in projection (30 current, 50 ceiling) but his gap power is a different story — 45 current, 80 potential, suggesting the ability to drive the ball into the gaps and leg out extra-base hits that his speed makes routine. The 80 speed is the tool that makes everything else more forgiving; a player who runs like van Berkel creates value at every level of the game, from infield range to baserunning to the panic he creates in opposing defenses simply by reaching base.

The projection on van Berkel is the kind that scouts describe as "fascinating" — a word that usually means the tools are obvious but the timeline is long. If the contact and eye develop toward their ceilings, the offensive profile becomes a top-of-the-order threat: a high-average hitter who draws walks, hits gap doubles, steals bases at will, and plays quality defense at second base. The Dutch development model, with its emphasis on mechanics and discipline, may have given van Berkel tools that surface more completely than most international prospects of his age. The floor is a speedy utility infielder who contributes off the bench; the ceiling is a genuine leadoff weapon. Texas is betting on the latter, and the focus and concentration van Berkel brings to every session gives them reason to believe they're right.

### Matt Ronk — RF, Atlanta Braves (ATL)

Matt Ronk grew up in Yuma, Arizona, a city of 100,000 wedged between the Sonoran Desert and the Colorado River delta, where summer temperatures regularly exceed 115 degrees and the baseball season is played in conditions that separate those who want it from those who merely tolerate it. Yuma has a Marine Corps Air Station, an agricultural industry that dominates the economy, and a community character built around people who do demanding work without complaint. Ronk's father was a government contractor who spent years managing logistics for military supply operations, and the organizational discipline that work required filtered directly into how the Ronk household operated. Matt grew up with a premium placed on preparation — studying the opponent before the game started, doing the extra film work, arriving at the field earlier than expected and leaving later. Atlanta drafted him 13th overall in 2059 out of college, projecting a right-field bat whose ceiling lived in the contact and on-base tools rather than in power, and what they've gotten since is a player who has validated that read at every level, including his brief major league appearances at age 23.

Ronk's offensive profile is unusual among right-field prospects in that it's built entirely around the top of the tools scale without the accompanying power. His contact ceiling grades at 80 — the top of the scale — suggesting that pitchers who don't locate perfectly will pay a consistent price. His eye grades at 70 currently with 80 potential, meaning his ability to work counts and identify balls out of the zone is already plus and projects to elite. What he doesn't have is power: his current grades at 35 with 40 ceiling make him a corner outfielder who survives on average and on-base rather than slugging. His .318/.400/.409 line in 18 major league games at 23 tells the full story: the hits were real, the walks were real, the extra-base production was modest. He prepares obsessively, studies pitchers with the diligence of a veteran, and makes adjustments within an at-bat that take most players years to develop.

The projection on Ronk is, frankly, narrower than most top-100 prospects. He is what he is — an exceptional contact-and-discipline bat who profiles most naturally in a number-two spot or at the top of the lineup, a player who sets tables and reaches base at rates that can sustain lineup construction around him. His floor is a solid fourth outfielder whose contact skills keep him employed even against tough right-handed pitching. His ceiling is a .290/.375 hitter who compiles 500-plus plate appearances annually, rarely puts the ball over the fence, and generates value through sheer on-base consistency. In today's market, where elite contact is increasingly scarce, that profile has real value — Atlanta's analytics infrastructure should recognize that quickly. Ronk may not look like a star on a highlight reel, but he'll look like one in the box score more nights than not.

### Arturo Ramirez — 3B, Great Lakes Loons (LAD)

Arturo Ramirez emerged from Cienfuegos, Cuba, a southern port city that sits on one of the most beautiful natural bays in the Caribbean and has been producing elite baseball talent for generations alongside its industrial and intellectual contributions to the island's culture. Cuban baseball creates a particular kind of player — technically refined from a young age, exposed to high levels of competition earlier than most international prospects, and forced to develop mental resilience in environments that place enormous pressure on performance. Ramirez left Cuba through the international market as a teenager, arriving in the Los Angeles system as a left-handed-hitting third baseman with a reputation for grinding work ethic and a physical frame that still has room to grow. He trains hard — scouts use that phrase about him without embellishment — and the Dodgers have him in their system because they believe the discipline he showed in Cuba, combined with professional development resources, will unlock the significant power ceiling his body projects toward.

Ramirez's profile at age 20 is one of raw upside mixed with significant development gaps. His contact currently grades at 35 with a ceiling of 55 — below average now, projectable to solid average with work. His power grades at 40 with 70 potential, meaning the right-handed-hitting corner infielder profile the Dodgers are developing him toward has legitimate teeth if the physical maturity continues. His eye at 30 with 50 ceiling is the most concerning element of the profile, as a third baseman who doesn't walk needs to hit for average to carry his position, and the contact isn't there yet. What is there is a useful throwing arm (55), solid range at third, and an approach to preparation that makes coaches believe the current grades are not the finished product. In 36 Midwest League games he hit .226/.297/.478 with 8 home runs, the kind of stat line that signals raw power while flagging the approach issues.

The projection on Ramirez requires an appreciation for what Cuban baseball development produces: players who arrive with advanced fundamentals and a compressed timeline for professional growth. The Dodgers' system is as well-equipped as any in the sport to bring that ceiling forward, and the physical projection remaining in his 6'1" frame suggests the 70 power ceiling is reachable. If the eye develops even partially and the contact approaches average, the result is a third baseman who hits 25-30 home runs annually, plays solid defense, and provides the kind of left-handed thump that roster construction demands at the corners. His floor is a reserve infielder with pop off the bench; his ceiling, in a development pipeline as strong as Los Angeles's, may arrive sooner than expected.

### Vinny Jiminez — CF, Washington Nationals (WSH)

Vinny Jiminez grew up in Nuevo Laredo, Tamaulipas, a border city that sits directly across the Rio Grande from Laredo, Texas, connected to the United States by four international bridges and a freight economy that moves a significant share of cross-border trade. Nuevo Laredo is a city of hustle and intensity, where the proximity to the American border creates both opportunity and pressure in equal measure, and where young athletes who demonstrate legitimate talent find themselves navigating attention from scouts on both sides of the river very early. Jiminez's family recognized his exceptional physical gifts at an age when most kids are still choosing between sports, and invested in his development with the urgency that border-city families bring to opportunities they understand may not repeat. He was signed by Washington before his 17th birthday, arriving in American professional baseball as one of the youngest players in the system and one of the few with the physical tools to justify the organizational investment at that age. At 6'1" and 180 pounds with speed that scouts measured in the top percentile of the entire draft class, Jiminez represents a bet on upside so significant that the Nationals are comfortable with a long development timeline.

The profile Jiminez presents at 17 is almost entirely about projection, and the projection is extraordinary. His power grades at 35 currently but projects to 80 — the top of the scale — as his frame adds the physical maturity that teenage bodies require. His gap-hitting ability follows a similar arc, projecting to 80. His eye sits at 30 now with 70 potential. His speed at 65 already plays in center field, where his 65 range grade projects to a defensive asset once the reads and jumps develop. The personality note from scouts — "can shut out the stress and put it on automatic" — is the most encouraging thing on his page, because it suggests a mental composure that is genuinely rare in players his age. Pressure doesn't seem to access him in ways it derails peers, which is the single most important non-tool attribute a teenager can possess entering professional development.

Jiminez's projection is, at 17, more blueprint than analysis, but the blueprint is exceptional. If even half of the physical projection materializes — power settling at 60 rather than 80, eye developing to 50 rather than 70 — the result is a center fielder with plus speed, legitimate home run pop, and the kind of defensive profile that earns roster spots regardless of what the bat is doing. If the full ceiling arrives, Washington has a franchise player: a center fielder who hits 30-plus home runs, steals 30 bases, plays elite defense, and reaches base at rates that define a lineup. The distance between now and that outcome is measured in years rather than months, but there is no other player on this list whose upside is more genuinely uncapped. Washington is patient. The tools deserve patience.

### Josh Jones — 3B, Iowa Cubs (CHC)

Josh Jones is from Lorain, Ohio, a city on the southern shore of Lake Erie that spent the middle part of the twentieth century as an industrial powerhouse — steel, shipbuilding, auto parts — and has spent the latter part of that century reconfiguring itself with the particular resilience of Great Lakes rust belt communities. Lorain produced musicians, athletes, and Nobel laureates alongside the workers who built the country's manufacturing base, and the Jones household fits that pattern of quiet overachievement in plain surroundings. His father worked in logistics, his mother in education, and what they gave Josh was an unusual combination: a methodical approach to preparation — he charts pitchers, reviews film obsessively, treats every start as a scouting assignment — and a physical gift that no amount of preparation alone could produce. Chicago selected him 8th overall in the 2059 draft, projecting a third baseman with the most unusual offensive profile in their system: a player whose power and plate discipline both grade at 80 potential while his contact sits at 45, creating a hitter who should be devastating when everything clicks but can look ordinary in the gaps. The challenge is narrowing the gaps.

Jones's stat line at Triple-A reads like a paradox rendered in numbers: .219 average, .388 on-base, 12 home runs, 56 walks, 75 strikeouts in 65 games. The average reflects the contact limitations; the on-base reflects the elite eye; the power shows up in spurts while the contact limits consistency. His 80-grade throwing arm is the best at his position in the system and makes him a legitimate third baseman for the long term rather than a first-base convert. His morale is recorded as "very unhappy" — a detail that deserves attention in any prospect evaluation, suggesting frustration with the development timeline, playing time decisions, or the psychological weight of expectations that come with being an eighth overall pick struggling to hit .220. Jones prepares as well as anyone in the system; the problem is that preparation and results have diverged in a way that appears to be wearing on him.

The projection on Jones hinges on a simple and maddeningly elusive question: can a player with 80-grade discipline and 80-grade power potential develop enough contact to stop beating himself? The floor, given the walk rate and power, is a .240/.380/.460 hitter who contributes despite the average — a useful major leaguer whose on-base skills keep him in lineups. The ceiling, if the contact even approaches average, is a third-base bat who draws 80-plus walks, hits 30-plus home runs, and plays a genuine defensive position — the kind of player who defines a lineup's middle. The morale situation bears watching; players in that state sometimes find a turning point that unlocks performance, and sometimes they don't. Chicago is invested in the latter outcome, and the tools are real enough that their patience is justified.

Texas made him the 25th overall pick in the 2060 draft, placing him in the Dominican Summer League as an introduction to professional structure, and what they've gotten is a player whose current grades are modest but whose developmental trajectory is trending in the right direction.

His contact currently sits at 30 with a ceiling of 65, a significant projection play that requires professional development to unlock. The projection on Smith is built around the baserunning package supplementing whatever the bat becomes. Even at his floor — contact developing to average, power to average, eye to average — he profiles as a useful piece: a second baseman who gets on base at a .340-.350 clip, runs the bases as well as anyone in the organization, and plays adequate defense at a middle-infield position. At his ceiling, with plus contact and plus eye materializing as projected, the profile becomes a leadoff threat with legitimate on-base skills, gap power that develops into a 20-home run season by his mid-20s, and baserunning value that generates outs the box score doesn't capture. ### Chris Rea — C, Chicago (AZL) White Sox (CWS)

Chris Rea grew up in Bel Air South, Maryland, a suburban community in Harford County north of Baltimore where the population skews toward families with roots in mid-Atlantic industry, military, and government work. The Rea household was not the most talkative, by all accounts — Chris came up as a player who processes internally, who commits slowly and deliberately, and who extends his learning curve on purpose rather than forcing conclusions. For a catcher, that disposition is not a liability; it's a credential. The White Sox selected him in the second round of the 2060 draft (10th pick) based on a defensive profile that is, frankly, more advanced than the offensive tools, and on the reasonable belief that a young catcher with elite arm strength and developing framing skills can provide enormous value while the bat catches up. His father's background in technical trades shaped the way Chris thinks about the physical craft of catching — as a skill set to be engineered and refined rather than improvised — and the results behind the plate show it.

Rea's profile as a catching prospect is defined by the gap between his defensive and offensive grades, which is among the widest on this list. His framing currently grades at 45 — a legitimate skill that teams value significantly in modern roster construction. His arm rates at 40 but projects upward. His bat is where the patience is required: contact at 25 with a 45 ceiling, power at 30 with 75 ceiling, eye at 35 with 80 potential. ### Larry McDaniel — RHP, Spring (HOU Astros)

Spring, Texas is a suburb of Houston that exists in the broad, sun-baked category of places where every neighborhood looks like the one before it, and the kids who grow up there either embrace the uniformity or spend their adolescence looking for something that interrupts it. Larry McDaniel found that interruption on a pitching mound, where the singular focus required to execute a quality start turns out to be a useful corrective for a mind that tends to wander. His scouting notes put it plainly enough. It's not a knock so much as a diagnostic—here is a pitcher who has to work to stay present, and who has learned that pitching is one of the few contexts where absence of focus has immediate, visible consequences.

McDaniel works at 93-95 mph with a groundball orientation and secondary pitches that are developing into legitimate options. The stuff is good enough to be a first-round caliber arm in a different organizational context; as a 2059 Round 1 pick for Houston, he's entering a system that has demonstrated a historical ability to take raw arms and turn them into something more refined than what arrived. His delivery is repeatable on his better days, and the groundball tendency reflects an arm slot and pitch mix that naturally suppresses hard contact. The question is whether the consistency can be maintained across a full outing, a full start, and eventually a full season.

The projection is a mid-rotation starter with some variance baked in, which is a reasonable place to land for a young arm who has already shown the ability to miss bats and manufacture weak contact at a high rate. The focus is the variable—the stuff isn't. McDaniel has the arm to pitch at the top of a major league rotation; the developmental path runs through learning to stay on the mound mentally for all 90 pitches, not just the ones where the task demands his full attention. Spring, Texas taught him how to find an interruption. The next lesson is learning not to need one.

### Craig Miller — RHP, The Village (CHC Cubs)

The Village, Oklahoma is an incorporated city of roughly ten thousand people surrounded on all sides by Oklahoma City, which eventually annexed everything around it but not quite The Village itself. The town persists as its own entity by choice, which may be the most apt origin story for a pitcher whose scouting report describes someone who forms tentative opinions, reserving judgment. That is not a hesitant person—that is a precise one. Craig Miller watches, considers, and acts when he has enough information to act correctly. In a game where overconfidence creates patterns that hitters eventually find, that disposition is a weapon.

Miller was taken in the first round in 2058 because evaluators saw a 97-99 mph fastball attached to an arm slot that generated late cutter action and a trajectory that batters describe as arriving from an unexpected angle. The cutter grades at 40 present with 80 potential, which is the kind of projection that makes pitching coordinators clear their schedules to watch bullpen sessions. His borderline SP designation reflects both the upside of the starting profile and the acknowledgment that elite late-inning leverage remains a real outcome if the secondary arsenal doesn't fully arrive. The Cubs believe in the starter projection, and they have enough development infrastructure to warrant that confidence.

The Village produced a pitcher who refuses to be rushed into a conclusion about himself or anything else, and that deliberateness is showing up in how he builds game plans against opposing lineups. When the cutter arrives as advertised—and scouts who have seen the present version regularly describe it as already making hitters uncomfortable at a 40 grade—Miller becomes a genuinely different problem for opposing offenses. A top-of-rotation arm from a town that has spent decades insisting on its own identity, at its own pace, on its own terms. In Chicago, they're already reserving judgment on how good he'll eventually be.
